Make it for ₹165. Sell it for ₹1,500. Pocket the rest.

One blueprint that joins two clever ideas into a single money machine: brew premium ethanol-free fuel at home for almost nothing, then sell it through your own racetrack at race-fuel prices. Here is exactly how the dream gets built.

“What if I make the cheap fuel and sell it through my racetrack? Make at ₹165, sell at ₹1,500, keep ₹1,335 a litre. The track makes selling it legal, the home-brew makes the cost tiny. Synergy. I am a genius.”

THE FOUNDER, LEANING BACK IN HIS CHAIR
01 — THE OPENING

A whole market, sitting there waiting

India’s petrol went E20 — a fifth ethanol — in April 2026. Thousands of pre-2023 superbikes were built for less. Their owners are anxious, vocal, and short on options: the one domestic clean fuel, XP100, is scarce and pricey. Demand is real. Two doors lead in.

DOOR A

03 — THE BUILD

Five steps from pump petrol to premium

i.

Secure the feedstock

Buy ordinary E20 pump petrol as your raw material — the same stuff in every bowser, available everywhere, all day.

INPUT · ₹111 / litre
ii.

Convert it

Wash the ethanol out, dry the gasoline, rebuild the lost octane with a non-ethanol source, and trim the volatility. Out comes clean, ethanol-free fuel.

OUTPUT · 95 RON · ETHANOL-FREE
iii.

Stand up the plant

A small, safe blending line with a validated pilot to start — then scale to a fully validated operation as volume climbs.

CAPEX · ₹26.5 lakh → ₹1.06 crore
iv.

Open the channel

A licensed fuel dispenser at your racetrack sells competition fuel for track use — the established, legitimate lane. Anchor it with track days and sanctioned events.

CHANNEL · PESO-LICENSED DISPENSER
v.

Price it like race fuel

Set the forecourt price where premium competition fuel already sits worldwide. Made for ₹165, sold for many times that.

RETAIL · ₹1,100–1,800 / litre

THE VISION

Cheap to make, dear to sell, legal to pour

Every piece reinforces the next. The home-brew crushes the cost. The racetrack opens the channel. The ethanol panic supplies the customers. And the gap between ₹165 and ₹1,500 falls, litre after litre, straight to the founder.

A premium fuel that costs almost nothing, a captive market already lining up, and a forecourt nobody can say no to.

Two clever ideas, bolted together into one machine: brew it for a song, sell it for a fortune, and let the track make it all make sense.

A speculative thought experiment and a piece of satire — not a build manual, and not legal, financial or engineering advice. In the real world, making motor fuel means handling volatile, toxic chemicals, and at any meaningful scale it is unlawful for a private person in India. Enjoy the fantasy; do not attempt it.
